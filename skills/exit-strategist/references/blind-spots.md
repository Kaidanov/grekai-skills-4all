# Blind spots — the factors that quietly decide whether an exit plan survives

The seven viral prompts model income and time. Real exits fail (or detonate) on the factors
below, which the prompts never ask about. Treat the **hard gates** as blockers to check *before*
building any plan — a plan that ignores them isn't optimistic, it's negligent. Treat the rest as
adjustments that move the timeline, the runway, or the right move entirely.

## Hard gates (check these FIRST — they can void the whole plan)

- **Visa / immigration status.** For anyone not a citizen/permanent resident, a work visa is often
  tied to the *specific employer*. Quitting, or doing self-employed work on the side, can breach or
  void it — sometimes triggering loss of residency. This outranks every financial number. If the
  user is on any sponsored/tied visa, that is the first question, and the plan must route around it
  (e.g. switch employers, change visa class, or earn only in ways the visa permits). Tell them to
  confirm with an immigration lawyer before anything.
- **Employment-contract clauses.** Many contracts contain: a **non-compete**, an **IP-assignment**
  clause (the employer may own things you build on their time/equipment — or broadly), a
  **moonlighting / outside-activity** ban, and **conflict-of-interest** rules. A side business in
  the same field, or built with any employer resources, can be a breach that costs the job *and* the
  side income. Read the contract before client work; build in a clean-room way; get legal eyes if the
  side path overlaps the employer's domain.
- **Resignation vs. dismissal asymmetry.** In most countries voluntary resignation forfeits
  unemployment benefit and statutory severance, while dismissal/redundancy preserves them (see
  `country-profiles.md`). "Just quit" can throw away months of safety-net income. Negotiating an exit,
  or being made redundant, is often financially far better — factor this into *how* they leave, not
  just *when*.

## Financial structure the prompts skip

- **Borrow while you still have a salary.** Banks lend against pay slips, not against a 6-month-old
  business. Mortgages, refinancing, and large loans get dramatically harder the day after you leave
  employment. If a mortgage or major credit is anywhere on the horizon, sequence it *before* the exit.
  This is one of the most expensive mistakes people make and almost no one mentions it.
- **Kill high-interest debt before "investing in a hustle."** A guaranteed 15–20% saved by clearing
  a credit card or consumer loan beats almost any speculative side-income return. Downside-first.
- **Liquidity ≠ net worth.** Being asset-rich (home equity, locked pension) but cash-poor is a trap;
  runway is measured in *accessible* cash, not paper wealth.
- **Concentration & platform risk.** Side income riding on one client, one platform, or one algorithm
  is fragile — account bans, algorithm changes, and a single client leaving can zero it overnight.
  Favor diversified demand and owned channels (email list, direct clients) over rented ones.
- **Currency risk.** Earning in a different currency than you spend (e.g. USD income, local expenses)
  adds volatility that can swing the "freedom number" 10–20%. Note it; don't pretend the rate is fixed.
- **Inflation-adjust the freedom number.** A fixed target silently shrinks every year. Index it.

## The retirement hole (the true cost of leaving)

Leaving employment isn't only this year's income. It's: lost employer pension/retirement
contributions, possibly years of lower self-funded contributions during the "building" phase, and
gaps in qualifying years for state pension / national-insurance benefits — all compounded over
decades. Quantify the long-term cost, not just the monthly gap, so the decision is honest.

## Re-employability & optionality (the off-ramp)

The plan must assume it might fail. Protect the ability to go back:
- Keep skills current and the professional network warm; don't let the résumé go cold.
- Leave well — burned bridges close the boomerang door. How you exit affects re-entry.
- Prefer reversible moves (sabbatical, part-time, internal transfer, leave of absence) that let you
  test the water without forfeiting the option to return.

## Behavioral & relationship realism

- **Energy, not just hours.** "20 hrs/week" after a full-time job and family is usually fantasy.
  Model sustainable effort (often 5–10 focused hrs/week), and the burnout risk of stacking a second
  job on the first. Plan for motivation decay — most side projects die at months 2–3.
- **Distribution is the bottleneck.** Most people can build the thing; few can get attention and
  customers. Many online income models silently assume a *pre-existing audience*. If there's no
  audience, customer acquisition is the real project — price the time and cost of it.
- **Escaping vs. running toward.** If the driver is burnout or a bad manager, the fix may be a
  different job, a transfer, or a sabbatical — not entrepreneurship. Diagnose the "why" before
  prescribing an exit.
- **Partner buy-in is a gate, not a nicety.** Income exits that the partner didn't genuinely sign
  off on strain or end relationships. If finances are shared, the partner is a stakeholder in the
  runway, the risk, and the home-labor shift. Surface this explicitly.

## Life-stage, dependents & geography

- **Age / runway-to-retirement.** A 25-year-old and a 55-year-old have opposite risk math; tailor
  aggressiveness to how recoverable a failed exit is at their stage.
- **Dependents' costs rise.** Kids' (especially education) and eldercare costs scale *up* over the
  plan horizon — don't model them flat.
- **Childcare trade-off.** Sometimes one parent's "side hustle" earns less than the childcare it
  displaces — or staying home is itself the financially rational move. Run both directions.
- **Cost-of-living arbitrage.** Relocating somewhere cheaper (or remote-first) can extend runway more
  than any side hustle — a powerful lever the threads ignore entirely.

## The third option (it's not binary)

"Stay miserable" vs. "quit and risk everything" is a false choice. Always put the middle paths on the
table: internal move, sabbatical, going part-time, negotiating terms, switching employers, "coast/
barista" semi-retirement, or — most underrated — **using the job as the funding engine** that
de-risks the build. Often the correct recommendation is *don't quit yet*, and that's a win, not a
failure of the plan.
