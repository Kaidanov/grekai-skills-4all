# Base rates — anchoring projections to reality, not to motivation

Use these as the gravity that pulls every projection back to earth. The viral genre is built on
**survivorship bias**: the person posting replaced their salary, so the algorithm shows you them and
hides the 95 who didn't. Anchor to the distribution, not the highlight reel. These are directional
reference ranges, not precise statistics — when a specific claim matters, web-search current data
and cite it; the point here is to stop the model from inventing motivating but false ramps.

## The shape of side-income reality

- **Most side projects earn little, for a long time, then many stop.** A large share never reach
  meaningful recurring revenue; a meaningful share are abandoned within the first few months. Plan
  for a long flat start, not a hockey stick.
- **Time-to-first-money** for service/skill work (freelancing, consulting, tutoring): often weeks to
  a few months *if* the user already has the skill and a way to reach buyers. For audience-dependent
  models (content, courses, products): commonly **6–18+ months** before non-trivial income, because
  the real work is building distribution.
- **Time-to-replace-a-salary** is the rare outcome. When it happens fast it almost always rides on a
  pre-existing audience, prior capital, an unusually scarce skill, or an existing client pipeline.
  Absent those, full replacement is typically a **multi-year** project, if it happens at all.
- **"Passive income" is mostly front-loaded active income.** The passive part comes after substantial
  upfront work and usually still needs maintenance. Treat "passive" claims as red flags to interrogate.

## Realistic ramps by path type (use as honest defaults)

- **Sell an existing skill as a service** (fastest feedback, lowest fixed cost): first money in weeks
  if buyers are reachable; scaling is limited by the user's hours — it trades time for money, so it
  caps out. Good for funding the runway; poor as a final destination.
- **Productized service / small B2B offer:** months to traction; better margins than pure freelancing;
  needs a repeatable acquisition channel.
- **Content / audience → monetization:** slow (6–18+ months), high variance, distribution is the whole
  game; most never monetize meaningfully. High ceiling, low base rate.
- **Digital products / courses:** require an audience or paid acquisition first; "build it and they
  come" almost never works.
- **Marketplace/platform gig work:** fast to start, but commoditized, capped, and exposed to platform
  and AI-pricing pressure.
- **Capital-based income (dividends, rentals):** scales with capital, not effort — usually irrelevant
  to someone trying to replace a salary from a standing start without significant savings.

## Sanity checks to run on any projection

- **The audience question:** does this model assume an audience the user doesn't have yet? If so, the
  first 6–12 months are an audience-building project with near-zero income — say so.
- **The hours question:** is the income target divided by a realistic *sustainable* hourly rate even
  achievable in the hours they actually have after job + family? If the math needs 30 hrs/week, it's
  fiction.
- **The survivorship question:** is this projection based on what a typical person achieves, or what
  the one viral success achieved? Default to typical.
- **The churn question:** does the model assume every customer stays? Build in realistic drop-off.
- **The competition question:** if it's easy and lucrative, it's crowded and getting more so —
  especially anything an AI tool now does for near-free (see `ai-displacement.md`).

## How to present numbers

Always give **three bands** and name the assumption that flips each:
- **Conservative** — slow start, high churn, limited hours actually materialize.
- **Base** — the honest expected case for a typical motivated person.
- **Stretch** — things go well; explicitly the *less* likely case, not the planning default.

Plan the household's survival around the **conservative** band. Let the base band set expectations.
Treat the stretch band as upside, never as the budget.
