# Country profiles — localizing an exit plan

The viral "escape your job" prompts silently assume a US worker: gross numbers, employer-tied
health insurance, 1099 side income, no statutory severance. That math is wrong almost
everywhere else. Before projecting anything, localize on these five axes. Always **state the
assumptions you used**, and **web-search current figures** — thresholds, benefit durations and
rates change yearly, so treat the numbers below as structure, not as live values.

## The five axes (ask these of any country)

1. **Currency & cost-of-living** — do all math in the user's currency at local prices.
2. **Tax on side income** — marginal rate, and the threshold where casual income must become a
   *registered* business (with bookkeeping, VAT/GST, social contributions).
3. **Safety net if the job ends** — unemployment benefit (eligibility, duration, % of salary),
   statutory severance, notice period. This sets how large the emergency runway must be.
4. **Health coverage** — is it tied to employment? If yes (notably the US), losing the job
   means losing coverage, so the runway must also fund insurance. If universal (Israel, most of
   Europe), this risk is far smaller.
5. **Pension/retirement continuity** — what employer contributions stop on exit, and whether the
   user must self-fund to avoid a gap.

## Israel (worked example)

- **Currency:** ₪ (NIS). Work in net (after-tax) shekels.
- **Side income / self-employment:** below a low annual turnover ceiling a person typically
  registers as **עוסק פטור** (VAT-exempt small dealer, still reports income tax & National
  Insurance); above it, **עוסק מורשה** (charges/reports VAT). Any recurring side income should be
  registered — "hobby income" that becomes regular is taxable. Confirm current ceiling and rules
  with a רואה חשבון / יועץ מס.
- **Safety net:** **דמי אבטלה** (unemployment) via the National Insurance Institute (ביטוח לאומי)
  — eligibility depends on qualifying months and age, paid for a capped number of days, *not*
  available to someone who simply resigns voluntarily (resignation usually disqualifies, dismissal
  qualifies — a key reason "negotiate an exit" can beat "just quit"). Statutory **פיצויי פיטורין**
  (severance) generally apply on dismissal after a year. Notice periods are set by law/contract.
- **Health coverage:** universal via the קופות חולים / National Health Insurance — **not** tied to
  the employer. Losing a job does not lose healthcare. This meaningfully shrinks the required
  runway versus the US.
- **Pension, פיצויים & קרן השתלמות:** employer pension contributions and (if any) study-fund
  (קרן השתלמות) contributions stop on exit; a self-employed person has a legal obligation to
  self-contribute to pension and can open a self-employed קרן השתלמות for tax-advantaged saving.
  **Severance (פיצויים)** has favourable tax treatment up to a ceiling and can be spread (פריסת מס);
  leaving timing affects the tax year — worth a יועץ מס before resigning. Early withdrawal of
  pension/קרן השתלמות before the permitted age carries tax and erodes the retirement base — model
  that cost, don't treat locked balances as free runway. On **emigration**, pension/קרן השתלמות
  handling and tax-residency change need cross-border tax advice (see `macro-and-relocation.md`).
- **Reserve duty (מילואים):** a real, often-overlooked income disruptor — the earner can be called
  up for weeks/months with partial compensation, removing them from both job and side hustle.
  Build a reserve-duty buffer into the runway and prefer income that survives the operator being
  away (recurring/productized/partner-covered). See `macro-and-relocation.md`.
- **Macro:** the NIS can swing with security and political events; USD-denominated income is a
  partial hedge but adds FX risk. War/security periods hit discretionary, in-person and tourism
  income first; remote/export work is more resilient.
- **Runway guidance:** health is covered and severance/unemployment may apply *if dismissed*, so
  the floor runway is essentials + pension self-funding + a reserve-duty/security buffer; a
  voluntary resignation forfeits unemployment, so model that explicitly.

## United States

- **Currency:** USD. The original prompts' home turf.
- **Side income:** reported on Schedule C / 1099; self-employment tax (~15.3% SS+Medicare) on top
  of income tax — a brutal surprise the threads omit. Quarterly estimated taxes once it's real.
- **Safety net:** unemployment insurance is **state-run, time-limited (commonly ~26 weeks)**, and
  generally **unavailable to voluntary quitters**. No federal statutory severance.
- **Health coverage:** usually **employer-tied**. Leaving means COBRA (expensive) or ACA
  marketplace — the runway MUST fund premiums. This is the single biggest US-specific risk.
- **Retirement:** 401(k) employer match stops; consider rollover. No forced self-funding.
- **Runway guidance:** larger than elsewhere — add 6–18 months of health premiums to the floor.

## United Kingdom

- **Currency:** GBP.
- **Side income:** the trading allowance covers a small amount tax-free; above it, register for
  **Self Assessment**; VAT registration above the turnover threshold.
- **Safety net:** statutory redundancy pay (length-of-service based, dismissal only), statutory
  notice; Universal Credit / contribution-based JSA as a thin floor; resignation limits support.
- **Health:** NHS, **not** employment-tied — low risk, smaller runway.
- **Retirement:** auto-enrolment pension employer contributions stop on exit.

---

## EU (generic — verify per member state)

- **Currency:** mostly EUR; local prices vary widely.
- **Side income:** most states require registering as self-employed/freelancer with social-
  contribution obligations that start early — often heavier than the US. Check the specific state.
- **Safety net:** generally **stronger** — longer unemployment durations and severance in many
  states (dismissal-based), but again resignation usually disqualifies.
- **Health:** typically universal/insurance-based, **not** employer-tied — low runway risk.
- **Retirement:** state + occupational; employer contributions stop on exit.

## India

- **Currency:** ₹ (INR).
- **Side income:** taxable; GST registration above the turnover threshold; "moonlighting" may
  breach an employment contract — check the contract before any client work.
- **Safety net:** thin formal unemployment support; gratuity after 5 years' service; EPF
  (Provident Fund) is portable. Effectively the **emergency fund is the safety net** — size it
  generously.
- **Health:** largely **private/out-of-pocket or employer-provided**; losing employer cover is a
  real cost — fund private health insurance in the runway.
- **Runway guidance:** lean toward a larger floor; informal safety net means savings carry the risk.

---

## For any country not listed

Web-search the five axes for the user's country (current year), state each assumption explicitly
in the plan, and flag what you couldn't verify. When unsure, **size the runway up, not down** —
the cost of an over-large emergency fund is small; the cost of an under-sized one is the whole
plan collapsing.
