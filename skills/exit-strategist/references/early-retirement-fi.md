# Early retirement / Financial Independence (FI) — the capital model

Use this when the user's destination is "retire early" / "never *need* to work again," not just
"replace my salary with a side hustle." It's a different machine: instead of building earned income
to match expenses, you accumulate invested capital until a safe withdrawal from it covers expenses.
Run this in place of engine steps 3–7, then return to the stress tests (steps 8–9).

> Not financial or investment advice. These are planning frameworks and rules of thumb, sensitive to
> assumptions; returns are uncertain and past performance doesn't predict the future. Anything
> binding — investing, pension, tax — confirm with a licensed advisor in the user's country.

## The core numbers

- **FI target capital ≈ annual expenses ÷ safe withdrawal rate (SWR).** The common rule of thumb is
  ~4% (i.e. ~25× annual expenses), derived from US historical data over 30-year retirements. Treat
  it as a *starting estimate, not a law* — it's more fragile for very long (40–50yr) early
  retirements, for non-US markets, and in low-return regimes. For a long early retirement, a more
  conservative 3–3.5% (≈28–33× expenses) is the honest planning range. State the rate you used.
- **Annual expenses** = the *household* number you actually want to live on, after tax on
  withdrawals, inflation-adjusted, including future care costs (kids, parents) and healthcare.
- **Years to FI** is driven overwhelmingly by **savings rate** (share of income saved), far more
  than by income level or investment return. Roughly: a ~50% savings rate implies on the order of
  ~15–17 working years to FI from zero; higher savings rate, fewer years. Compute it from their real
  numbers rather than quoting the cliché.

## The lighter variants (usually the realistic answer)

- **Coast-FI** — they've already invested enough that, with *no further contributions*, normal
  market growth reaches a full FI number by a normal retirement age. From that point they only need
  to cover *current* expenses, so they can downshift, take a lower-stress job, or let one partner
  carry the household. Compute the Coast-FI number for their age and target date — many people are
  closer to this than to full FI.
- **Barista-FI** — a smaller portfolio plus a modest part-time income (often kept partly for
  benefits/health/pension coverage) covers the gap. Lets them leave the high-stress job decades
  before full FI.
- **Blend with income-replacement** — side income shortens the accumulation runway and de-risks
  sequence-of-returns. Most real exits are a blend, not pure FI.

## The honest caveats to always raise

- **Sequence-of-returns risk.** Retiring just before a market drop can permanently damage a
  portfolio; a string of bad early years is far worse than the same years later. Mitigate with a
  cash/bond buffer (1–3 years of expenses), flexible spending, or a small ongoing income.
- **Healthcare & insurance** across a multi-decade retirement (see `country-profiles.md` — minor
  where health is universal, major where it's employer/private-tied).
- **Inflation** compounds brutally over a 30–50 year horizon; the target must be real, not nominal.
- **Behavioral risk.** Decades of high savings is hard; lifestyle creep and life events derail it.
  Plan for flexibility, not heroic frugality.
- **Locked vs. accessible capital.** Pension/retirement wrappers may be tax-advantaged but
  restricted until a set age — an early retiree needs a *bridge* of accessible (taxable) capital to
  cover the years before locked accounts unlock. Model the bridge explicitly.

## Localize the wrappers

Tax-advantaged accumulation differs by country (e.g. US 401k/IRA/brokerage; UK ISA/SIPP; Israel
קרן השתלמות / קופת גמל להשקעה / pension). Use the right local vehicles and note their access ages and
tax treatment. For Israel specifics and the emigration angle, see `country-profiles.md` and
`macro-and-relocation.md`. When unsure of current limits/rates, web-search them and state the date.
