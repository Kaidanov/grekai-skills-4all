# Macro shocks & relocation — planning under instability

The viral prompts assume a calm country and a fixed location. For users in unstable or
geopolitically exposed regions, **involuntary macro disruption** can wreck an exit plan as surely as
losing the job — and **relocation** is often a bigger lever than any side hustle. Apply this at the
stress-test step, alongside the AI displacement test, and surface relocation as a first-class option
in the plan.

## Part 1 — Macro & country-shock stress test

Model the shocks that are realistic for the user's country, then check the plan survives them.

- **War / security / unrest.** Can shut sectors, freeze hiring, close businesses, and make
  in-person income impossible for periods. Tourism, retail, construction and discretionary services
  get hit first; essentials and remote/export work are more resilient. Favor income that doesn't
  depend on local foot traffic or physical presence.
- **Mobilization / reserve duty (e.g. Israel מילואים).** This is the one the prompts never imagine:
  the earner can be called away from *both* the job and the side hustle for weeks or months, often
  with little notice and partial compensation. Effects: build an extra **reserve-duty buffer** into
  the runway; prefer income that keeps running while the operator is away (recurring/productized,
  delegable, or a partner-covered service) over income that stops the moment they stop working;
  factor it into every timeline, since a plan assuming 50 uninterrupted weeks a year is fiction for
  someone in the reserves.
- **Currency risk & inflation.** A volatile local currency swings the value of both savings and
  any foreign-currency income; a depreciating currency erodes the freedom number. Earning in a
  hard/foreign currency while spending locally can be a hedge — or a risk if it reverses. Hold part
  of the buffer in a stable store of value; state the FX assumption.
- **Capital controls / banking risk.** In severe scenarios, access to money can be restricted. For
  high-instability countries, keeping some accessible, diversified, and (where legal) jurisdiction-
  diversified savings is prudent. Don't over-engineer this for stable countries.
- **Concentration on one fragile economy.** If job, side income, savings, and home are all tied to
  one shaky economy, the household has no diversification at all. Relocation, foreign-currency
  income, or remote clients abroad each reduce that single-point risk.

Then run the contingency explicitly: *"If there's a 2-month security disruption / reserve call-up /
20% currency drop, does the household stay above its floor?"* If not, enlarge the buffer or
restructure the income before scaling anything.

## Part 2 — Relocation as a strategic lever (three flavors)

Relocation can extend runway, cut risk, or unlock opportunity faster than any hustle — but each
flavor has tax, visa, pension, currency, and family consequences. Put the relevant ones on the table.

- **Domestic relocation (cost arbitrage).** Moving to a cheaper city/region can cut the freedom
  number sharply with no visa/tax complexity. Lowest-friction lever; often underrated.
- **International relocation / emigration.** Can address cost, safety, opportunity, or tax — but
  triggers: visa/residency rules, tax residency change (and possible exit tax), what happens to
  **pension** contributions and withdrawals across borders, healthcare re-enrolment, currency
  exposure, kids' schooling, partner's career, and distance from aging parents. Treat as a major,
  partly irreversible move requiring professional (immigration + cross-border tax) advice.
- **Remote-first (decouple income from location).** Keep or build income that travels — remote
  employment, foreign clients, productized services — so the household can relocate (or weather
  local shocks) without rebuilding its income. Often the smartest first step: it buys optionality
  before any physical move.

## Part 3 — Sequence the irreversible moves

Quitting, relocating, and launching a business are each partly irreversible and each stressful.
Doing all three in the same quarter multiplies risk and burns the off-ramps at once. Stage them:
usually **build remote/portable income first → prove it → then relocate or quit**, keeping the
partner's salary and the re-employment option intact until the new structure is real. For families,
the partner and (where relevant) children must genuinely be on board — a contested relocation or
exit strains the household as much as a failed business does.
